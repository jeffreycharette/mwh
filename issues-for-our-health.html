For Our Health

Florida Healthcare

In 2013, the Florida House of Representatives chose to reject $51 billion in federal money to pay for the expansion of Medicaid to cover over 1 million poor Floridians who currently have no health insurance coverage. The federal subsidy would have paid 100% of the costs of expanded coverage for the first three years and at least 90% of costs thereafter. In place of Medicaid expansion the House offered a plan that would use state funds to give recipients $2,000 a year to choose their own private insurance plans. The plans would cover fewer people, require a $25 monthly premium, and have high deductibles. Most of those eligible for the plans would not be able to afford to either purchase or use them. Adding insult to injury, Florida's House members, who work two months a year, pay only $8 a month for their own much more comprehensive health plans.

From an alternate reality: MARY LYNN MAGAR, the current representative from District 82 said the House plan is far better than Medicaid. She said it will allow recipients to “take control of their own medical coverage” to choose their own plans and “see the doctor that they want to see.”

Florida's legislators and Rick Scott's administration also sought to derail the expansion of health insurance coverage by seeking to sabotage the rollout of the Affordable Care Act. They declined to set up a state-run exchange, turning down federal money to offset the cost."

Florida's legislators and Rick Scott's administration also sought to derail the expansion of health insurance coverage by seeking to sabotage the rollout of the Affordable Care Act. They declined to set up a state-run exchange, turning down federal money to offset the cost. Scott's administration drummed up an identity-theft bogeyman and used it to bar ACA navigators from setting foot on Florida Health Department property. The legislature suspended the Office of Insurance Regulation's right to review and regulate rates for new health insurance plans for two years in the hope that high rates would be blamed on "Obamacare."

Turning to the NRA for health care advice, in 2011 the legislature passed and Governor Scott signed the Firearm Owners Privacy Act (also known as Docs vs. Glocks) which prohibited doctors from asking patients about gun ownership. The law was struck down in court but taxpayer dollars are being spent on an appeal.

Florida needs to reform its dysfunctional ways in healthcare. Mary is a Medicaid provider with 23 years experience working with Medicaid, Medicare, Social Security programs, and KidCare. She has extensive firsthand knowledge of the scope and interactions of these programs. She is a Waiver Support Coordinator who works as an independent contractor for the State of Florida, providing intensive case management for adults and children with developmental disabilities. These disabilities include intellectual disabilities, cerebral palsy, autism and Prader-Willi syndrome.
