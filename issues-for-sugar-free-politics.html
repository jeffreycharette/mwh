For Sugar-Free Politics

Do Republican Politicians Still Represent Republican Voters?

Traditional Republican voters have a painful reality to face. The Republicans in Tallahassee are now Teapublicans. The Florida Republican Party has been taken over by a well-funded radical fringe which is focused on representing special interests. The Tea Party's knee-jerk anti-government, anti-public services rhetoric has taken Florida into territory that would have been dismissed as irresponsible just a few short years ago.

The financial crisis of 2008 provided an excuse to slash environmental spending. Huge cuts were made to Everglades restoration and funding for the Water Management Districts and Florida Forever was gutted- its funding cut by 97.5%. Now that surpluses appear likely to return, Teapublicans favor keeping and expanding tax giveaways for well-connected businesses rather than restoring investment in the environment. Crucially, on October 12, 2013, the Scott administration failed to exercise the expiring option to purchase sugar lands needed for a flow way south at a reasonable price and with bargain interest rates available.

Consider the Republican's recent record: The Republicans have eliminated all capital investment in Florida's K-12 public schools, giving that money only to charter schools instead. Private education interests donated over $2,000,000 to political campaigns in 2012- practically all of it to... Republicans.

Republicans put the Children's Services Councils on the ballot so we would have the "opportunity" to defund programs like Head Start, ARC, Big Brothers Big Sisters, Boys & Girls Clubs, Tykes & Teens, and the YMCA.

Florida charged to the forefront of the movement to revive Jim Crow laws by reducing early voting opportunities, implementing partisan voter purges, and creating voter id laws that are intentionally burdensome for many poor and minority voters.

The Florida legislature turned down $51 billion in federal aid to expand Medicaid coverage to over 1 million poor Floridians who currently have no health care coverage. Florida's share of the expansion would have been nothing for the first three years and 10% of the cost thereafter. Tea Party politicians evidently believe we are so selfish that we are unwilling to spend anything at all to help the less fortunate and so foolish that we can't see that we're already spending that money on higher insurance premiums to fund charity care in emergency rooms.

Putting partisan politics ahead of the needs of the people, the Republicans sought to sabotage the rollout of the Affordable Care Act."

Putting partisan politics ahead of the needs of the people, the Republicans sought to sabotage the rollout of the Affordable Care Act. They refused a grant to set up a state-run marketplace and discouraged people from using the federal marketplace by offering misleading price comparisons of newly available plans to hypothetical plans while ignoring the cost-savings of the subsidies that are an integral part of the ACA. Rick Scott's administration created an identity-theft bogeyman which it used as an excuse to prevent ACA navigators from helping people obtain health insurance at any Florida Health Department facilities. Similar navigators have helped people sign up for Medicare, Medicaid, and KidCare for years. Not content with creating impediments to making healthcare readily available, the Republicans also chose to make it more expensive. They suspended the Insurance Commissioner's authority to review and approve health insurance rates on new plans for two years, hoping that unfettered, unregulated rate increases will be blamed entirely on "Obamacare."

The Republican's sorry record on healthcare would be incomplete, however, without a mention of their recent foray into the doctor/patient relationship in the infamous Docs vs. Glocks bill. This law would have fined or suspended licenses of Doctors who asked patients if they had guns in their homes. When the courts ruled the law unconstitutional, Florida spent taxpayer money on an appeal.

Florida's traditional Republican voters have some thinking to do. They need to decide if the current Republican Party really represents their values and interests.
